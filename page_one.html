On July 10, 2016, a 334-ton reactor pressure vessel was accidentally dropped from a height of 2-4 meters at the Astravyets Nuclear Power Plant construction site. The information about the droppage would have remained confidential If not for the noise generated by the object’s mass. The geographical closeness of the yet to be finished Nuclear Power Plant to Lithuania resulted in registering the light seismic activity of unidentified nature in Vilnius. The intense noise generated by the vessel was no louder than the deafening silence of the Belarusian officials who tried to conceal the accident. The crane malfunction and the miscalculations in the strength of transporting slings resulted in a sag with a subsequent droppage. After the Facebook publication of an anti-nuclear activist Nicolai Ylasevich went viral, officials had to react (Ylasevich, 2016). What followed was a publication of a statement by Alexander Lokshin, the first deputy general director of Rosatom, claiming that the vessel was not damaged and no deformations that could result in the change of its properties were detected. Noone from the Belarusian State Production Association of Electric Power Industry “Belenergo” commented on the situation.

While the officials in Belarus were trying to nullify public discontent, it was decided that the dropped vessel in which “no deformations that could result in the change of its properties were detected” would be replaced. However, during transit, the new vessel for the Astravyets NPP had hit reinforced concrete support of the contact network of the Belarusian railway station “Slavnoe.” In the official statement that followed, it was claimed that the transport company representatives, insurers, manufacturers and other organizations examined the shell and concluded that it was not damaged. However, the experts of the Belarusian Anti-Nuclear Campaign have indicated the inconsistency in the official version. It was claimed that the vessel was transported in a protective casing. The released photographs showed that there

was no protective casing on the vessel. Fueled by the overall mistrust towards the government, Belarusian activists demanded the re-examination of the vessel. According to the international nuclear energy specialists, if the integrity of the vessel is violated, then the effective cooling of the core will be disrupted. Such disruptions can lead to overheating and potential accidents. Nevertheless, the vessel was delivered and installed. The public re- examination of the vessel has never been carried out.

Russia itself plays a key role in the NPP project. Firstly, Russia provides significant financial investments. In 2011, Belarus and Russia signed an intergovernmental agreement about allocating funds for Astravyets NPP. According to the agreement, Russia will cover 90% of the 10 billion dollars project while Belarus will cover only the remaining 10% of the entire costs. Secondly, Russia provides technological investment as the Russian State Atomic Energy Corporation Rosatom functions as the general constructor. The main segments of the plant, two WWER-1200 (water-water power reactors) with 1200 megawatt (MW) output each, were developed and assembled by a Russian state construction office working on the design and development of nuclear power plant reactors of the WWER range called Hydropress Design Bureau. The reactor vessels were produced and transported to Belarus from Atommash, an engineering company located in Volgodonsk, Rostov region, Russia. The vessels for Astravyets NPP became the first vessels produced by Atommash after 30 years of inactivity. Thirdly, Russia provides the resources for the plant. Belarus does not have natural uranium resources, so Russia, with its estimated 500,000 tons of uranium deposits in the South Urals, Western Siberia, and Siberia east of Lake Baikal, will be the key importer. Before being delivered to Belarus, the uranium for Astravyets NPP will undergo the enrichment process at the Novosibirsk Chemical Concentrates Plant, which is also impossible without strategic cooperation with Russia.
